New NUSMODS

Last edited on 10 November 2018 by Shengmin Yang.

Overview

I am a frequent user of NUSMODS. NUSMODS is a timetable tool widely used by NUS undergraduates to plan their modules. Although it has been continuously improved, it still lacks some important features that NUS students desire, such as adding reminders to the timetable and deriving a common time slot for a group of people. As users of the existing web application, we have also found common pain points amongst each other and amongst our friends. However, for the purpose of this project and the user-centered design approach we have adopted, we have made sure to detach ourselves as users, and adopt the designer hat.

We aim to add new convenient functions to NUSMODS to improve it as a tool for the planning for both academic and personal events, and to provide an integrated platform for NUS Students to organize their timetables. Additionally, our goal is to reduce the frustration that arises when students encounter certain problems like being unable to find a common slot with their friends or their timetables not being able to sync across multiple devices.

The project started with the team brainstorming and coming up with plausible functions that can be incorporated into the new web application. The detailed functions are listed below. Initially, we have also decided to come up with a new name for the web application: NUS Moments.

We came across the following new functions.

Signing In/Out: The signing in and out function will mean that each nus email account is associated to a respective timetable on the web application. With this new feature, common problems like timetable discrepancies across multiple devices and browsers will be reduced. It also aids in some of our other functions like timetable comparisons and booking facilities.

Timetable Comparison:The timetable comparison function enables easy comparison between different students’ timetables to list out common time slots. There will be two views: Comparing timetable with one person and comparing timetables with between 2-10 people. If the students want to have a discussion during a common slot, they can be directed to the booking facilities page to view available discussion rooms.

Book Facilities: One-click booking of sport facilities and study rooms will also be incorporated. Instead of struggling to get their various bookings via different websites, students can get all the troublesome work done at one common site. After a facility has been successfully booked, the booked time slot is automatically added to the booker’s timetable and optionally sent to his/her teammates through email.

Study Groups and Interest Groups: Another function that we plan to put forward revolves around study groups, which facilitates discussion among students who are studying for the same module or external exam such as CFA (Chartered Financial Analyst). It is worth noting that students who have enrolled in modules alone may find it difficult to find a study partner. Although the “forum” function is available on IVLE for some modules, it is not popular or pervasive among students. Hopefully, the study group function can gather groups of like-minded individuals who are striving towards common academic goals. In the same vein, the interest group function gives students the opportunity to join either official NUS interest groups or unofficial meetups for sports, music or arts events.

Add Your Own Event: Adding your own event was also raised as a useful function as it will provide a more accurate means of timetable comparison and students are able to add events like Consultations or external school related non-academic events.

Reminder Function: Lastly, the vast number of events on the timetable, either academic or personal, necessitates the remainder function. Users can mark and receive reminders for the events they deem as important.

Project Duration: The project duration is during Academic Year 18/19 Semester 1: September 2018 – November 2018.

Based on the initial few functions that were derived, we proceeded to conduct interviews with current NUSMODS users and verified these findings through online surveys.

Preliminary Research

Before conducting an in-depth study of the problem, we performed a quick sanity check by asking others around us if they too face similar issues. Surprising, only a few had troubles with finding study groups, but most have problems finding places for group meetings.

This is owed to the fact that senior students have a group of well-known friends to study with. Many students prefer to take the same modules as their friends.

We also visited the places where the discussion rooms are located and checked whether advance booking is required.

User Research

Target User: As our new project NUS Moments will be built upon the current NUSMODS whose users are mainly NUS students, the main user group for our website will largely be the same. We have identified two main user groups: undergraduate students and postgraduate students. Exchange students and visiting students are excluded from the user group, since their stay at NUS is too short for them to significantly benefit from NUSMODS and they have lower needs to plan for modules & set reminders for exams. Whilst different groups of students may have different usages and opinions of NUSMODS, we started out our project with user study in order to precisely define our target group.

The research is structured as follows:

  • i) Semi-structured interview: Upon defining user groups, we carried out a series of interviews with 8 randomly selected NUS students across campus where we had them described their experiences with NUSMODS and their opinions on the proposed new features. The purpose of the interviews is to find out the current user experience for NUSMODS and areas in which they would like to see improvements in.
  • ii) Contextual Inquiry: Following the semi-structured interviews, it is necessary to witness the behaviors of the users to confirm that the issues persist among them. Out of the 8 interviewees, 2 were randomly selected for contextual interview. We observed these two people using current NUSMODS and talking with their friends about the proposed new features.
  • iii) Surveys: After analysing the interview results, we further surveyed 30 randomly selected NUSMODS users to validate our interview findings.

Semi-structured Interview

At the end of the survey, I invited three respondents to an interview to share their experiences based on their survey responses. The aim is to explore in-depth their lives, habits and the difficulties they faced.

The interviews conducted with the said participants are designed to be semi-structured. These are one-to-one interviews guided by prepared questions but remained open to being framed by their responses during the interview. As such, questions are likely to change and be adjusted in the process to ensure that problems encountered by users emerge from the users themselves.

The following sums up the interviewees' encounters, what they want and why.

The participants proceed to demonstrate their points through a contextual inquiry exercise, in which they would carry out several tasks as a group. I went to tag along and observe, taking down important pieces of information along the way.

By borrowing subjects of the same demographic from my interview, I am able to relate to their experience first-hand with the goal of building a deeper level of empathy of their problems. Should I be in doubt, I would raise questions directed towards their thoughts and feelings on-set. However, I needed to ensure that the process is non-intrusive to avoid any Hawthorne Effect where subjects knowingly performed due to my presence.

The task is specified as follows:

I gathered a few friends to put together a short video to demonstrate the problems that were observed during the contextual inquiry. I figured out that the best way to do this is to emulate a similar scenario infused with some comical element.

The process was further mapped to a user journey map where I would break down and analyze each step in greater details. A user journey map is useful in highlighting the touchpoints a user has with the current method of securing a karaoke place from start to finish. It describes how a user thinks and feel in every scenario of the journey, revealing more insights into their mental models.

Research Tools

Affinity Diagram

To make better use of the feedback gathered from my studies, I consolidated and categorized them into themes denoting a goal or problem into one place. Designers would usually refer to them as affinities where each affinity is captured onto a sticky note which would be moved around and grouped together. The idea here is to draw patterns to help determine the next actionable step.

Empathy Map

After acquiring information from different types of users, I can now make use of the newfound learning to create an empathy map to represents the group of users that I will be designing for. The empathy-mapping process is known to help designers distill and synthesize their knowledge of users into one place. It is the collective voices and needs of those that I have interviewed. Doing so establishes a common understanding about the user, ensuring that a product stays in focus with the user’s needs. At the same time, it also enables the discovery of factors that drove the users’ behavior and other unexpected insights.

An empathy map is divided into four plus two quadrants, outlining notes on six different aspects (feelings, sights, actions, influences, problems, and goals), thus producing an overview of the person’s experience.

Survey

The purpose of conducting a survey is to examine the different contexts a person would visit a karaoke place. Questions include the number of guests, choice of venue, timing as well as navigation methods. You can find the complete set of survey question here. Surveyees were also asked on the type of problems encountered and possible suggestions to improve the situation. Such questions were open-ended to collect qualitative feedback that were based on experience rather than perception. By designing the survey in this manner, respondents can better recall relevant experience followed by specific events that left a deep impression on them.

The survey was disseminated online through personal contacts and social media (basically channel that are known to be cheap and quick) with the following demographics kept in mind.

Of those whom I approached, 50 of them responded with the faction comprising of both working adults and university undergraduates. Below is a breakdown of the result gathered from each question.

Survey Findings

From the survey findings, I found out that:

  • Most respondents tend to frequent a karaoke place with four to six other people in the afternoon (3 - 5 pm) with each session lasting 2 to 3 hours. The most popular joints to visit were Teo Heng KTV Studio and Party World KTV due to its sheer number of outlets nationwide.
  • Current methods of finding a karaoke place are passable with most ratings lying between 4 to 6, with 10 being the easiest, suggesting much room for improvement. Locating a karaoke place is usually done based on prior experience (35%) or using navigation apps (34%). A handful of them (20%) also preferred finding their way around themselves with the help of mall directories situated in districts.
  • Booking a karaoke room is also far from perfect, scoring an average difficulty rating of 5.12 out of 10. Users generally suffered from missing details or unreachable hotline when making a phone inquiry.
  • Of all the booking methods presented, 63% chose to make phone calls, whilst 28% would rather to walk in without any prior reservation.
  • Despite the complications in booking, a majority felt that securing a suitable timeslot is relatively easy judging by the average difficulty rating of 8. The success rate is however, subjected to time and the popularity of the venue.
  • Other problems were brought up as well. Examples include vendor-related issues (i.e., price, operating hours, etc.) as well as UX flaws like the inability to track booking records.
  • When questioned if an app is useful in solving their problem, 56% were in favour of the idea, 36% remains sceptical and 8% rejected the initiative. The responses should however, be taken lightly as they are based on the participant’s existing knowledge about applications rather than the intended product. A product can only be accurately evaluated after released.
  • Considering everything, respondents gave an overall satisfactory rating of 7.5/10 (10 being very satisfied) for their experience with karaoke place (from searching all the way to arriving at the location).

User Personas

Using the empathy map, it is possible to transfer the information about the user to what is known as personas. By introducing specific traits and personalized experience to personas, I can create fictional characters whom my product would serve.

Each persona includes the basic element of the map areas, as well as additional, specific information about the imagined user.

Product

Concept

With the overarching idea of assisting users with securing a place to sing, I embarked on a mobile application concept, titled G6 to address the identified problems in my earlier studies. G6 is named after a song called Like a G6 by Far East Movement due to its punk-ish vibe that took the world by storm back in 2010. The song is quick to rise in popularity, becoming one of the most requested songs to sing-along at clubs, karaoke, and disco bars.

It is the goal of G6 to bring together all aspects of visiting a karaoke place in one place. The idea is to streamline the process of finding and booking a karaoke place without having to rely much on multiple sources and applications – think of it as a one-stop hub for all Karaoke needs. The app is designed based on the following principles derived from the user responses.

i) Display results according to price and distance

Being the primary concern of the users, the app needs to introduce some ways to enable categorization and sortation based on meaningful metrics (i.e., distance and price) to hasten a search. For it to be effective, it needs to work with a sizeable result generated within a certain radius. Other filters such as popularity and amenities are equally important as well.

ii) Deals, promotions and discounts

Due to the overwhelming request for promotional campaigns and the common adoption of such practices, it is decided that G6 should have a dedicated section featuring the latest deals and promotions. With the idea of Kiasuism, often regarded as the fear of losing out to others being so prevalent in Singapore, it is not unusual to see people trying so hard to stay abreast of the latest news and promotions. Comparing prices across options has become part of the purchasing process. Users are quick to dismiss something as ‘expensive’ if they manage to find something cheaper elsewhere.

To accommodate this behavior, it helps to consolidate and flesh out a list of ongoing programmes which users can take advantage of. However, the terms and conditions of each campaign need to be explicitly stated to avoid disappointment. To take the idea of discount further, the app can consider the use of promo codes issued by participating vendors when checking out.

iii) Accurate and up-to-date information

Users generally hate the feeling of not being delivered the experience as promised on the internet. As such, it is important to present information that is salient to the users in a precise and cohesive manner. Factors such as user reviews, photos of the venue and featured amenities are what set the expectation about a place prior to a visit. Therefore, such content needs to be carefully curated and organized in a way that can be easily understood without any incongruity. Partnering vendors should also work together with designers to ensure that their information stays up to date especially for dynamic information such as date, time and prices.

iv) Provide direction and location on map

Many have expressed concern with navigation as GPS apps are sometimes confusing and unreliable. However, designing a brand-new way of navigating could introduce a steep learning curve especially when users are already familiar with how apps such as Google Maps or Apple Maps operate. Knowing this, it would be useful to tap on their existing knowledge using geo-location APIs that focuses on information relevant to a location. Users can expect to find the same information like address and directions as they usually would on a navigation app.

v) Ease of Booking and Management

Despite users not having many qualms about booking, it is a necessary feature for the app to be functional. The process should be kept as simple as possible, asking for only minimum information necessary for booking. As a general rule of the thumb, the process should be kept within five steps to ensure an optimal experience.

The paranoid nature of individuals like Gary Wong (User Persona 2) requires the app to contain a record of their booking. This is to provide some form of assurance regarding their purchases. Users may need to occasionally refer back to information like timeslot and price when prompted from time to time. The app should cater to post-booking woes by granting them the ability to manage their booking and performed any last-minute action like cancellation. This is also one of Nielson Jakob’s usability heuristic, user control and freedom – which designers need to pay attention to when designing.

Storyboarding

To visualize how the core features of G6 would work, I drew a general workflow of each feature on a storyboard using pen and paper. A storyboard is a canvas broken down into a sequence of images arranged in chronological order. It illustrates the steps taken by users to achieve their goals. By breaking down a complex idea into small, procedural steps, designers can envision themselves as the users and reveal any flaws in their thinking.
